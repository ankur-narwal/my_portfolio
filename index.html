How I Built My Chess Website

Building Chess Wars: My Journey of Creating a Complete Chess Website

Introduction


Chess has always fascinated me—not just as a game of strategy, but also as a way to bring people together. As a developer, I wanted to combine this passion with my coding skills and create something of my own: a chess website where anyone could log in, play, solve puzzles, and enjoy the game in a simple, user-friendly environment. This project became one of my most exciting learning experiences, and in this post, I’ll walk you through the journey.

Why I Started This Project


Like many computer science students, I’ve always wanted to work on projects that push my skills while still being fun to build. Creating a chess platform felt like the perfect match. It’s interactive, full of logic, and always keeps you thinking. And since chess has that timeless appeal, building a place where people can actually play and practice felt like I was working on something that truly matters.

The Features I Built


Here’s what I focused on while making Chess Wars:
● Play Locally – Two players can sit together and play on the same board. Perfect for quick matches with friends.
● Game Modes – Different ways to play (like puzzles and challenges) so the site doesn’t feel repetitive.
● Puzzles Section – I added 50 hand-picked chess puzzles to sharpen tactical skills.
● Tournaments & Chat UI – While not fully implemented yet, I laid the foundation for tournaments and a chat system to give the site a community feel.

Technical Side


Chess Wars was built with HTML, CSS, and JavaScript as the main stack.
● HTML – Structured all pages clearly, from the homepage to the puzzle section.
● CSS – Used for styling, responsiveness, and the glassmorphism effect.
● JavaScript – Powered the logic for the chessboard, move handling, and puzzle integration.

What’s Next?


Chess Wars is still evolving. I want to add:
● Online multiplayer so people can challenge friends remotely.
● Smarter puzzles and training modes for learners.
● Community features like leaderboards, chat, and tournaments.

Conclusion


Building my chess website was more than just a coding exercise—it was about bringing together creativity, problem-solving, and my love for the game. It’s a project I’m genuinely proud of, and I believe it reflects both my technical skills and my ability to design with users in mind.

How to Make Your Portfolio

How to Make Your First Web Developer Portfolio (That Recruiters Actually Read)

1) What this post will help you do


You’ll go from “I need a portfolio” to a clean, fast, recruiter-friendly site you can ship in weeks — not months. We’ll cover the whole flow: goal → theme → structure → projects → writing → build → launch. No fluff, just what gets you interviews.

2) Start with a clear goal (so you know what to show)


Pick one primary goal:
Internship / first job → show fundamentals, small but polished projects, ability to learn fast.
Junior front-end role → highlight UI craft, responsiveness, accessibility, performance, a couple of real users if possible.
Freelance → show outcomes and business impact (before/after, metrics, testimonials).
Everything you add should support that goal.

3) Choose a theme & design system (then stick to it)


A portfolio feels “premium” when the visual language is consistent. Decide this upfront:
Theme idea (example you can adopt): “Dark Neon Tech”
Background: near-black (#0e0e0e)
Text: pure white (#ffffff) and soft gray (#aaaaaa)
Primary: cyan (#00ffff) for accents, hover, borders
Accent: mint (#00ffcc) for secondary highlights
Radius: 12–20px rounded corners
Shadow: soft, subtle; never blurry glowing unless intentional
Motion: 150–250ms ease transitions; no wild parallax

Design rules

1–2 fonts max (e.g., Inter for UI, a display font if needed).
Use a 8px spacing scale (8/16/24/32…); keep paddings consistent.
Define 3 heading sizes (e.g., 40/28/20), body (16–18), small (14).
Keep contrast accessible (aim for WCAG AA).
Pick one button style; use it everywhere (primary/outline variations).
Lock this in, then stop tweaking colors every day. Consistency beats perfect.

4) Plan your structure (information first, visuals second)


A recruiter spends ~30–90 seconds on your site. Make it scannable:
Hero — Name, title (e.g., “Front-End Developer”), one line value prop, 2 buttons (Download Resume / See Work).
About — 4–6 lines: what you’re good at, what you’re learning, what you want next.
Projects — 3 strong projects > 10 average ones. Each with a quick case study.
Skills/Tools — grouped logically (Core, Frameworks, Tooling).
Writing (optional but great) — short posts or notes.
Contact — clear CTA; email + social.
Add a sticky header with simple nav: Home · Work · Writing · Contact. Smooth scroll. Active link states.

5) Pick a simple stack (don’t over-engineer)


Base: HTML + modern CSS + a little JS is totally fine.
Optional: React/Vite if you want component reuse; Tailwind if you love utility classes.
Deployment: GitHub Pages, Netlify, or Vercel (all free/easy).
Bonus: a tiny build step (minify CSS/JS, compress images).
Remember: the projects matter more than the framework.

6) Projects that earn interviews (what to build & how to present)


Choose 3 projects that show range

For each project page/card:
Title & one-line summary (“Interactive music app with playlists & search”).
Live demo + GitHub links.
Context: Why you built it (problem).
Approach: Tech choices, interesting challenges.
Outcomes: What improved, what you learned (be specific).
Next steps: if you had one.
Keep the writing human and concrete.

7) A simple case-study template you can reuse


Context
2–3 sentences: who it’s for, problem, constraints.
Goals
What “done” looked like (speed, UX, mobile, accessibility).
Stack
Front-end tech and why (short and honest).
Key challenges
Bullet the top 2–3 problems and how you solved them.
Results
Numbers if you have them. If not: tangible outcomes (e.g., “ran smoothly on low-end phones”).
What I learned
Specific takeaways. Shows growth mindset.

8) Build it clean (layout, responsiveness, performance)


Layout
Use a max-width container (e.g., 1100–1200px).
Generous white space; readable line-length (60–75 characters).
Grid for projects; stack to single column on mobile.
Responsiveness
Performance
Inline critical CSS, defer non-critical JS.
Avoid heavy libraries for simple effects.
Unique title and meta description per page/section.
Semantic tags: header, main, section, article, footer.
Open Graph/Twitter meta so shared links look good.

9) Accessibility (non-negotiable)


Proper heading order (H1 → H2 → H3).
Alt text that actually describes the image’s purpose.
Visible focus states (keyboard users exist).
Color contrast checked for text and buttons.
Don’t trap focus in modals; ESC should close.
This is the difference between “nice site” and “hireable engineer.”

10) Micro-interactions that feel intentional


Button hover: subtle scale (1.03) + border/shine; 200ms ease.
Link hover: underline offset or gentle color shift.
Cards: 4–6px translate-up on hover; modest shadow change.
Reduce Motion: respect prefers-reduced-motion.
Small touches make your site feel “designed,” not just “assembled.”

11) Content that sounds like you (not a robot)


Write short, active sentences.
Swap vague words for specifics.
“Built a responsive UI” → “Built a 3-breakpoint layout with CSS Grid/Flexbox.”
Use first person sparingly, and keep verbs strong.

12) A realistic build plan (2–3 weeks)


Day 1–2: Goal, theme, wireframe (mobile → desktop).
Day 3–6: Hero, About, and Projects grid.
Day 7–10: Case study pages/sections, performance passes, accessibility.
Day 11–12: Writing page or notes (optional), contact section.
Day 13–14: Polish: favicons, meta tags, 404, social preview images.
Day 15: Deploy + share + iterate.

13) Common mistakes (and how to avoid them)


Too many projects → Pick 3 and go deep.
Inconsistent styles → Decide once, reuse everywhere.
Walls of text → Break with headings, bullets, images.
Heaviness → 2–3MB total page weight target is reasonable.
No contact CTA → Make it obvious and friendly.

14) Final advice (that actually helps)


Treat your portfolio like a product: solve a problem (show your ability), deliver value (clarity + demos). You don’t need a perfect site; you need a clear one that proves you can build, learn, and polish.

If you follow this flow — set a goal, commit to one theme, choose three projects, write short case studies, and ship a clean build — you’ll already be ahead of most first-time portfolios.

Mastering Prompt Engineering


When I first came across the concept of prompt engineering, I was fascinated by how a simple change in wording could completely shift the way AI responds. It’s not just about asking questions—it’s about knowing how to guide the model so that the answers are accurate, creative, and actually useful. Over time, I explored different strategies like role-based prompts, zero-shot and few-shot techniques, and breaking down complex tasks into smaller steps. What I found most interesting was how even tiny tweaks—like choosing the right tone or adding a bit of context—could dramatically improve the results. I’ve applied these skills in practical settings, from research experiments to projects where prompts shaped the way AI interacted with users. For me, mastering prompt engineering has been about building a bridge between human intent and AI capability—making sure the output isn’t just correct, but also meaningful.
